Hakone 2-Day Trip: Hiking the Old Tokaido Road and a French Dinner at Hotel Biore

2024年9月27日
読了時間: 4分

“If we go to Hakone, I want to walk the old road.” That one line was all it took to set the plan in motion. September 27–28, 2024: one night in Hakone, Kanagawa. Instead of the ropeway or the pirate ship cruise on Lake Ashi, the main attraction for this trip was walking an actual stretch of the Old Tokaido Road—the stone-paved path that has remained almost unchanged since the Edo period.

Quick Facts

  • Dates: September 27–28, 2024 (1 night, 2 days)
  • Location: Hakone-machi, Ashigarashimo-gun, Kanagawa
  • Travel style: Hiking the Old Road + onsen accommodation
  • Hiking route: Hatajuku → Hakone Old Road → Motohakone (approx. 3 hours)
  • Accommodation: Hotel Biore, dinner included (French course)
  • Estimated cost (per person): Transport ¥3,000 / Accommodation (2 meals) ¥28,000 / Miscellaneous ¥2,000 → Total approx. ¥33,000
  • Weather: Overcast (late September, cool temperatures)

Day 1: Hiking the Hakone Old Road from Hatajuku to Motohakone

Getting to Hatajuku

We arrived at Hakone-Yumoto just after noon and transferred to the Hakone Tozan Bus, heading to Hatajuku—the starting point for the Old Road hike. Hatajuku is a small post town at the midpoint of the Hakone Old Road, surrounded by a quiet rural settlement. From here, it’s a three-hour walk along Edo-era stone pavement to Motohakone.

Tracing the Japan Heritage “Hakone Old Road”

After passing through the Sukumo River Nature Trail, we entered the main stretch of the Old Road. Explanatory signs—bearing the “Japan Heritage” and “Hakone Town” markings—appeared one after another, each naming a different slope along the way.

Past the Amazake-Chaya Area and into the Named Slopes

About 90 minutes into the hike, we passed the section near the historic Amazake-Chaya teahouse and entered the stretch of famous named slopes.

“Dannoki-zaka (Dannoki Slope)”—a Japan Heritage information board stands at the side of the road. This slope was one of the most notorious sections of the Tokaido during the Edo period; historical records describe it as “particularly steep, causing great suffering to many travellers.” Reading the sign made the ancient difficulty suddenly real.

An old green sign in English and Japanese—“旧街道石畳 Hakone Stone Paved Road”—leans at an angle in the undergrowth. The path is maintained, but its wildness is part of its charm.

We reached a fork in the path. A signpost indicated “Hatajuku 1.5km” and “Motohakone / Main Road.” Looking back, we had come 1.5 km from Hatajuku. The route ahead continues along the Old Road toward Motohakone.

Sarusuberi-zaka and Oikomi-zaka—The Notorious Slopes

Every slope on the Old Road has a name. “Sarusuberi-zaka” (Monkey-Slip Slope)—said to be so steep that even monkeys would slide. “Oikomi-zaka” (Chasing Slope)—a gradient so severe it felt like being driven into a corner. Even on today’s maintained path, the inclination those names describe comes through in your legs.

A wooden post for “Oikomi-zaka (OIKOMI SLOPE)” appears. Next to it stands a sign pointing to “元箱根 Motohakone.” We’re almost there.

The Cedar-Lined Stone Pavement—the Highlight of the Old Road

A large wooden gate appears, reading “Hakone Old Road / 40 minutes to Motohakone.” Beyond it lies the most photogenic stretch of the entire route.

The stone pavement stretches on. On both sides, towering cedars—30 metres or more—stand in a solemn procession, turning the path into something resembling a sacred approach. When this scene opened up in front of us—the same view we had seen in photographs—the fact that samurai processions once marched through here more than three hundred years ago suddenly became tangible.

Through the Mt. Futago Area, Down to Motohakone

After the cedar forest, we entered an area with information boards about Mt. Futago (二子山). Details on Hakone’s volcanic topography and vegetation, with a trail feel to the surroundings.

“Slope No. 16: Enoki-no-Genzaka”—the sixteenth slope counting from Odawara. A stone monument and wooden explanatory board stand here. The Old Road reportedly had 17 named slopes in total, each numbered from Odawara.

And then—Lake Ashi (Ashinoko) opened up before us.

Near the boat pier at Motohakone. Under an overcast sky, the quiet lake surface stretched out, surrounded by mountains. The harbour jetty was visible to the right. Three hours of walking had brought us here, and the satisfaction mixed with the lake’s silence kept us standing still for a while. Mt. Fuji was hidden in cloud, but the scene was beautiful enough without it.

Tonight’s Accommodation: Hotel Biore

A short walk from Motohakone brings you to Hotel Biore, tonight’s stay. The stone exterior gives it a European-hotel feel, standing quietly in the forest. After check-in and dropping luggage in the room, we rested in the quiet until the time for the hot spring and dinner.

Day 1 Evening: French Dinner Course at Biore

Dinner was a French course at the hotel restaurant—five courses, completely different from the kaiseki cuisine of a traditional ryokan. “French food in Hakone?” was a slightly unexpected thought, but any meal tastes good after a three-hour hike on the Old Road—and beyond that, the quality was genuinely impressive.

The first dish was a mixed appetiser plate. On a white rectangular plate: thin slices of carpaccio with caviar, a soft-boiled egg, marinated vegetables, a meat terrine, and a slice of apple—arranged neatly. The care in the plating was apparent in every element.

The pasta was tagliatelle (flat ribbon pasta) with meat sauce and spinach—a substantial, warming dish for the cool Hakone autumn.

The fish course was a white-fleshed fish with an orange-coloured sauce, accompanied by what appeared to be small taro-like rounds and a bonito-flake-style garnish.

The meat main—possibly pork or chicken braised in white wine vinegar—came with mushrooms and whole garlic cloves, a red wine-coloured sauce spreading across the plate. Served with mashed potato and broccoli, it was the most substantial dish of the course.

Dessert brought a chocolate cake, matcha ice cream, vanilla ice cream, and fruit on one plate. As a way to close a dinner after walking the Old Road, nothing could have been better. Including the hot spring bath afterward, the feeling of “I’m so glad we came” was beyond words.

Day 2 Morning: Hotel Breakfast Buffet

The next morning (September 28), breakfast at the restaurant buffet: a plate of sausage and poached egg, croissant and corn soup, salad and fruit. Simple choices, but well-balanced in quantity, and we ate slowly while looking out at the greenery.

After check-out, a short walk around the area, then the bus back toward Odawara. On the way home, we stopped for a cold draft beer.

Three hours on the stone road in the morning, a French dinner, a soak in the hot spring, a relaxed breakfast to close—this one-night trip was dense with experience. What started as a simple impulse—“let’s go to an onsen”—was lifted into something more memorable by the unexpected main event of the Old Road hike.

The Old Road is the kind of place you keep meaning to visit but keep putting off. Walking it in person was far more interesting than expected—every slope has a name and a story, and reading the signs as you go dramatically increases the depth of the journey. The ropeway and pirate ship cruise are great too, but if a friend asks about Hakone in the future, I’ll be recommending the Old Road hike first.
