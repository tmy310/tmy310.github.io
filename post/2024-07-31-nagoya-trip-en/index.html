Nagoya 2-Day Trip: Port Aquarium, Nagoya Castle & Nagoya Meshi (July 2024)

2024年7月25日
読了時間: 2分

Late July 2024, Nagoya in summer heat. The city is notoriously hot and humid in the dog days of summer, but the aquarium air conditioning and the stone corridors of the castle proved excellent counter-programming. A packed 2-day trip covering Nagoya Port Aquarium, Nagoya Castle, and the unavoidable Nagoya meshi food experience.

Quick Facts

  • Dates: July 25–26, 2024 (1 night, 2 days)
  • Location: Nagoya City, Aichi Prefecture
  • Access: ~1 hour 40 min from Tokyo by Shinkansen / ~50 min from Osaka
  • Cost (per person, estimate): Transport + accommodation + food + entry fees → approx. ¥25,000–40,000
  • Weather: Extremely hot (midsummer inland city)

Day 1: Nagoya Port Aquarium

The Aquarium as Summer Strategy

Midsummer Nagoya is merciless. The decision to make the aquarium the first stop was basically a survival choice: air conditioning. But Nagoya Port Aquarium turned out to be genuinely impressive—not just a cool place to hide from the heat.

The facility is split into a North Building and a South Building. The South Building houses the star attractions: orcas and dolphins, performing in an enormous pool. The South Building’s main tank holds shows with a scale and energy that filled the stands.

The dolphins were in their pool, and the crowd around the performance area was already building. The movement and size of the animals at close range is a different experience from a TV screen.

Then the orca pool. The scale of an orca in person is something that photos can’t fully communicate. These animals are significantly larger than most people expect.

The North Building covers deep-sea creatures, sea turtle breeding, and more. The tuna display—a school of large fish circling a cylindrical tank—was hypnotic.

Evening: Tebasaki (Chicken Wings)

Nagoya has its own distinct food culture—Nagoya meshi—and the most famous item on that menu is tebasaki: chicken wings seasoned with spicy salt and pepper, then coated in a sweet-spicy sauce. The contrast is addictive, and the pairing with cold beer makes perfect sense.

Nagoya tebasaki is not what most people expect from chicken wings. They’re deep-fried plain first, then tossed in the sauce, giving a combination of crispy skin, juicy meat, sweet-spicy glaze, and black pepper that’s unlike anything sold outside the city.

Day 2: Nagoya Castle

The Castle and the Honmaru Goten

Day 2’s focus was Nagoya Castle (名古屋城)—the headquarters of the Owari Tokugawa clan from 1612 until the Meiji period. The current tenshu (main tower) is a reinforced concrete reconstruction from 1959, but what makes the visit exceptional is the Honmaru Goten (本丸御殿)—a meticulous reconstruction of the inner palace completed in 2018.

The palace interior recreates the original sliding-door paintings (障壁画, shohekiga) and decorative schemes with an extraordinary level of craft. Gold leaf, vivid mineral pigments, and painted tigers and eagles on every surface—it’s designed to communicate the authority and cultural ambition of the Tokugawa clan to anyone who entered.

Inside the tenshu itself, each floor holds historical displays—armour, weapons, models of the original castle, and material about the Tokugawa clan.

Nagoya packs a lot into a compact geography. Two major attractions of genuinely different kinds—marine life and Edo-period history—plus a food culture that’s distinctive enough to be worth travelling for specifically. The only regret: not enough time for hitsumabushi (grilled eel over rice), miso katsu, and ankake spaghetti. There will have to be a second trip.
