Fukui 3-Day Trip: Dinosaur Museum, Eihei-ji, Tojinbo & Echizen Crab (Nov 2024)

2026年4月8日
読了時間: 4分

→ Planning: Fukui 3-day guide · → Japanese: 福井2泊3日の旅

Fukui, November 2024: dinosaurs, Zen, cliffs, and opening-week crab

March 2024: the Hokuriku Shinkansen finally stops at Fukui. I bookmarked the news and landed on 14–16 November, right after the 6 November crab opening, with one clear mission—eat Echizen crab in season—and three big side quests: Fukui Dinosaur Museum, Eihei-ji, and Tojinbo.

Four reasons at once meant there was no excuse left to postpone. The trip cleared the bar on all of them. The photo run is 179 frames end to end (castle town, Mikuni, station platforms on the way home) so you can scroll it like a travel log.

Quick facts

  • Dates: 14–16 November 2024 (2 nights, 3 days)
  • Area: Fukui Prefecture (Fukui City, Katsuyama, Sakai, Eiheiji Town)
  • Style: Business hotels, trains and buses
  • Rough budget (one person): Shinkansen return ~¥24,000; two nights ~¥18,000; Echizen crab ~¥15,000; local meals and transport ~¥10,000 → about ¥67,000 total
  • Access: Tokyo → Fukui on the Hokuriku Shinkansen (Tsurugi / Hakutaka), fastest about 2h06
  • Best timing: November (crab season) or April–May (cherry blossoms)
  • Weather this trip: Cloudy to sunny all three days—lucky for the Japan Sea side in November.

Day 1: Shinkansen to Fukui Station

Suddenly much closer by bullet train

Tokyo to Fukui now takes as little as 2 hours 6 minutes on the Hokuriku Shinkansen. Compared with the old limited-express connections past four hours, the island feels smaller.

Between Toyama and Ishikawa the window framed the Hakusan ridges. Almost every slice of the Hokuriku line could be a postcard.

We rolled into Fukui a little after 11:00. Past the ticket gates, a life-size tyrannosaur roared in welcome—touristy at first glance, but it is exactly how Fukui sells its dinosaur identity, and it grew on me fast.

Station area and dinner

After dropping bags at the hotel, we walked the refreshed plaza outside Fukui Station—a mix of visitors and locals in the post-extension buzz.

That night, a local izakaya: Echizen soba with a mountain of grated daikon (oroshi soba), the sharp radish lifting the buckwheat aroma, plus grilled mackerel—fatty, soy-dipped, perfect with sake. Early sleep before the heavy sightseeing days ahead.

Day 2: Fukui Dinosaur Museum & Eihei-ji

Dinosaur Museum: four times better than expected

Next morning: Echizen Railway and a connecting bus to Katsuyama. The Fukui Prefectural Dinosaur Museum ranks with Canada’s Royal Tyrrell and China’s Zigong as one of the “world’s three great dinosaur museums.”

The dome hits you first—life-size skeletons packed shoulder to shoulder: Tyrannosaurus, Triceratops, Fukuiraptor, and names I had never heard. Photos cannot carry the mass of real bone in front of you.

A 2023 renewal added new wings and hands-on exhibits. Kids leaned in at the dioramas; adults read every caption—maybe the adults slower than the kids.

Outside, Fukuiraptor statues dot the grounds—the whole campus feels designed as a dinosaur pilgrimage site.

Time: Allow 3–4 hours for a proper visit; half a day with special exhibitions.

Eihei-ji: silence from another world

Afternoon at Eihei-ji, founded in 1244 by Zen master Dogen and still home to over two hundred monks in training.

Past the approach, the air changes—centuries-old cedars, light on flagstones, creaking wooden corridors, monks moving with no wasted motion. Everything says this place is different.

You can tour the seven main halls (Dharma hall, Buddha hall, gates, sangha hall, kitchen, bath, latrine). Reading each sign, you glimpse Zen discipline—even kitchen and bath treated as practice. That idea is baked into the layout.

Access: Bus about 10 minutes from Eihei-jiguchi Station; coin lockers at the station. Time: Plan 1.5–2 hours on site.

Day 3: Tojinbo cliffs & Echizen crab

Tojinbo: cliffs that weaken your knees

Day three opened at Tojinbo in Mikuni—columnar basalt cliffs along the Japan Sea, a national natural monument. Over a kilometre of hexagonal columns stacked from ancient magma: rare at this scale anywhere in the world.

At the edge you look straight down 25 metres to the sea—less “pretty view,” more raw pressure. Windy days can feel like the wind wants you over the rail; the promenade is there, but use your judgement.

Crowds came and went, but the site is big enough to breathe. Sightseeing boats run below the cliffs—apparently the angle from the water is another league of drama.

Echizen crab: a main reason for the whole trip

6 November marks the season opening for Echizen crab (male snow crab landed in Fukui). That date was circled on my calendar.

We sat down at a seafood restaurant near Mikuni port for boiled and grilled crab. Thirty minutes per crab, and I was happy the entire time.

The meat is fine-fibred and sweet; the kani miso is absurdly rich and loves sake. A friend said they had had Hokkaido hair crab and king crab before, but Echizen was its own thing—I agreed. Expensive, but if you are in Fukui in November, skipping it would hurt more than the bill.

Mid-meal I realised this was the most expensive single dish of the trip—and simultaneously that I had zero buyer’s remorse, which almost never happens abroad. Those thirty minutes of picking meat and miso were the point of coming to Fukui.

Maruoka Castle

With time to spare we added Maruoka Castle, one of only twelve surviving original keeps in Japan. Small but heavy with age—dark roof tiles, steep wooden stairs, and from the top a wide view over the plain. Few visitors; we could linger.

It never felt crowded—easy to slow down.

Maruoka Castle town & Sakai streets (more photos)

After climbing the keep we kept shooting side streets and castle-town textures. Even a compact original tenshu photographs like a heavyweight.

Mikuni port, downtown Fukui, and the station

Post-crab, more shutters around the port and station—documenting the Hokuriku Shinkansen bookends of the journey.

Next morning: heading home

We closed day three, then caught the Shinkansen from Fukui back to Tokyo the next morning—short trip, high density.
