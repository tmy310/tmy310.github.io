Hakone 2-Day Guide: Old Road Hike, Lake Ashi & Onsen (2024)

2024年9月21日
読了時間: 4分

Hakone is one of Japan’s most accessible mountain-and-onsen destinations—just 90 minutes from Shinjuku. But with so many routes, passes, and accommodation options, deciding where to start can be overwhelming. This guide covers everything you need, based on an actual 2-day trip in September 2024.

For the full trip story, see → Hakone 2-Day Trip Report: Hiking the Old Tokaido Road and a French Dinner at Hotel Biore

Hakone Old Road Hike: A Highlight Worth Planning Around

One feature that often gets overlooked in Hakone itineraries is the Hakone Old Road (箱根旧街道)—a stone-paved stretch of the original Tokaido highway that remains largely intact from the Edo period (1603–1868).

Recommended Route: Hatajuku → Motohakone

SectionTimeDetails
Hatajuku → Amazake-Chaya area90 minNamed slopes including Sarusuberi-zaka, Dannoki-zaka
Cedar-lined stone road20 minThe most scenic stretch—great for photos
Mt. Futago area → Motohakone50 minFinal descent to Lake Ashi
Total~3 hoursModerate difficulty; trekking shoes recommended

Access to starting point: Bus from Hakone-Yumoto to Hatajuku (Hakone Tozan Bus)
End point: Motohakone Port, where you can take a bus or the pirate ship

What Makes It Worth Doing

  • Every slope on the route has a name and an Edo-period backstory. The Japan Heritage information boards (in Japanese) tell the history of each.
  • The famous cedar-lined section looks nothing like a typical hiking trail—it feels like a temple approach road.
  • The trail is accessible and well-maintained, but the stone can be very slippery after rain. Allow 3–4 hours at a comfortable pace.

What to Check Before Booking

  • Open-air private baths fill up fast—book 2–3 months ahead for weekends.
  • Spring type varies: sodium chloride (slightly salty), sulfuric (white milky), or simple alkaline (gentle on skin). Check the accommodation’s official site.
  • Meal quality (local ingredients, kaiseki vs. set menu) directly affects satisfaction. Always read recent reviews.

Off-peak tip: January–February weekdays. The hot springs are very uncrowded, and accommodation rates drop significantly.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Mistake 1: The Owakudani ropeway was suspended
→ Volcanic activity can cause sudden closures. Check the official Hakone Ropeway website before you leave.

Mistake 2: Couldn’t make the most of the Free Pass due to crowds
→ The ropeway queues longest in the afternoon. Start at Owakudani in the morning and head to Lake Ashi later.

Mistake 3: Couldn’t get a room in the desired area
→ Popular Hakone ryokan fill up for weekends 2 months in advance. Book immediately once your dates are set.

Mistake 4: Stone pavement was slippery and hard to walk
→ The Old Road stone becomes very slippery after rain. Trainers or running shoes are not ideal—trekking shoes or trail shoes with grip are strongly recommended.

Summary

Hakone is one of Japan’s most reliable options for a first-time short trip—transport, accommodation, sightseeing, and food are all well-organised. But going beyond the standard ropeway-and-cruise circuit to include the Old Road hike adds a completely different dimension: Edo-era history you can walk through, one step at a time.
