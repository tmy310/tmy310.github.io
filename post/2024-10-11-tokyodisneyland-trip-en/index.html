Tokyo Disneyland Halloween 2024: Buzz Lightyear, Beauty and the Beast, and a Full Day at TDL

2024年10月11日
読了時間: 4分

October 11, 2024. I spent a full day at Tokyo Disneyland (TDL) for the first time in years.

If I’m being honest, I had some hesitation going in. The tickets aren’t cheap, the lines are long, and part of me thought “isn’t this mostly for kids?” But someone invited me, and I figured—why not give it a shot. October is late Halloween season at TDL. That turned out to be the right call.

Basic Info

  • Visit date: Friday, October 11, 2024
  • Location: Tokyo Disneyland, Urayasu, Chiba Prefecture
  • Time in park: Approximately 9:00 AM to 8:00 PM (~11 hours)
  • Cost estimate (per person): Entry ticket ~¥9,400, food/drinks ~¥3,500, snacks/goods ~¥2,000 — Total: around ¥15,000
  • Access: 5-minute walk from JR Keiyo Line Maihama Station (about 15 min from Tokyo Station)
  • Crowd level: Late Halloween season = relatively manageable; major attractions 15–50 min waits

Arrival: Giant Pumpkins and Mickey Scarecrows

We got through the gates just before 9:00 AM. The first thing that hit us: massive orange pumpkins and a Mickey Mouse scarecrow figure flanking the entrance. The whole plaza was decked out in orange and purple for Halloween. I didn’t expect the decorations to be this thorough.

The moment you walk through the gates, the environment engineering kicks in. Everything is intentional. The Halloween atmosphere was fully committed—not a half-hearted banner here and there, but an entire color palette transformed. A few steps in and Cinderella Castle appeared in the distance.

Tomorrowland: Buzz Lightyear’s Astro Blasters

Our first attraction was Buzz Lightyear’s Astro Blasters in Tomorrowland—a seated shooting ride where you aim a gun-shaped controller at targets and compete for a high score.

The queue itself was impressive. The walls were covered in massive mural art: Buzz Lightyear vs. Emperor Zurg, columns of Little Green Men, signs reading “Complete the Mission.”

After the ride, our final score was displayed: 383,300 points. Far from the top rank “Galactic Hero,” but it was a decent showing. The thing about this attraction is that it’s genuinely fun—you’re actually trying, and there’s real satisfaction in hitting a good shot. We both wanted to go again immediately.

Westernland: Big Thunder Mountain Queue

Next stop was Big Thunder Mountain in Westernland. While waiting in line, we had an unexpected view: Cinderella Castle visible through the wooden fence boards of the Frontierland-style queue. A white fairytale castle framed by rough-hewn timber. The contrast was oddly charming.

The queue interior itself featured detailed mining machinery—ore carts, drilling equipment, wooden scaffolding—all with the kind of weathered realism that makes you forget you’re in a theme park.

Lunch: Ramen with a Mickey-Shaped Egg

Around noon, we stopped for lunch at one of the park’s restaurants. The main event: ramen with a Mickey Mouse–shaped fried egg on top. Chashu pork, vegetables, proper broth. The kind of meal that’s cute enough to photograph before you eat it, but actually tastes good.

TDL food gets a reputation for being overpriced—and it’s not entirely wrong. But when a bowl of ramen comes with Mickey’s ears stamped into the egg yolk, it’s doing something that no ordinary restaurant can do. The price includes the experience.

Afternoon: Theatre Orleans Show

Around 3 PM, we caught a performance at Theatre Orleans. Mickey Mouse, Donald Duck, and Minnie Mouse all on stage together, with live entertainment that had the whole audience engaged.

Even knowing intellectually that these are costumes and performers, something shifts when the characters are right in front of you, moving and interacting. I found myself clapping along without thinking about it. That’s the magic Disney parks do better than anywhere else.

Beauty and the Beast Area: The Beast’s Castle Blew My Mind

The highlight of the day—and probably the reason I’d go back—was the Beauty and the Beast area (New Fantasyland) and the Enchanted Tale of Beauty and the Beast attraction.

The Castle Exterior

Walking into the area, you’re confronted by a lavender-gray Gothic castle rising above an artificial landscape of cliffs and waterfalls. The central spire, flanked towers, limestone-textured walls—it’s the architectural language of European medieval fortifications, done with TDL’s commitment to total immersion.

The surrounding landscape is just as carefully built out: waterfalls cascade down sculpted rocks, creating a natural-feeling approach to the castle.

The castle facade features lion heads carved into ornate window frames. At the entrance, griffin (eagle-lion hybrid) statues and lion statues flank the pillars.

The Queue Is a Museum

The wait time for this attraction is typically 60–120 minutes. But here’s the thing: the queue itself is the experience. Each room of the castle queue is fully decorated to match the film’s interiors, and you move through them slowly enough to actually look.

On the fireplace mantel: Mrs. Potts (the teapot) and Lumiere (the candelabra) side by side. Above them, a coffered wood ceiling. Below, a heraldic shield with crossed sabers.

In front of the fireplace: a deep crimson velvet footstool on a floor of rose-patterned tiles.

Moving deeper into the queue: a lavender wallpapered gallery room with a chandelier and two framed flower paintings, plus a floor-to-ceiling medieval tapestry covering one wall.

Then a room with floor-to-ceiling red velvet curtains and an elaborately carved wooden desk—on top of which sits a Mrs. Potts teapot figure surrounded by dried flowers.

A corridor lined with full suit-of-armor knight statues on raised platforms, set against deep red damask wallpaper and marble columns.

Inside the Ride

On the approach to the ride vehicles: a stained glass window depicting a rose, lit from behind with deep blue lighting on a stone staircase. If you know the opening of the Beauty and the Beast film, this image lands immediately.

The kitchen section featured a Cogsworth (the clock) figure standing at a stone oven surrounded by copper pots and barrel furniture.

The ride itself is about 10 minutes—a spinning vehicle that tracks through scenes from the film, with a mix of screen projection and physical sets. It’s technically impressive, but what stays with me is that the queue built expectations so effectively that the ride felt like a payoff.

Snack: Mickey-Shaped Manju

After the ride, we picked up a Mickey-shaped sweet at one of the park’s snack stands. Wrapped in pink Disney packaging, it was a gyuhi (soft mochi-like confection) molded into a rose shape. Lightly sweet, chewy, exactly the kind of small treat that makes sense to eat while walking.

Evening: Cinderella Castle Lit Up at Night

Around 6:30 PM, the castle had transformed. Where the daytime castle is bright white, the evening version was illuminated in deep orange and purple—Halloween colors—with bare tree silhouettes in the foreground. It looked like a scene from a Gothic fairy tale.

I’d seen photos of TDL’s nighttime castle lighting, but the actual scale and color saturation are different in person. It’s the kind of visual that stops you in your tracks.

Dinner: Western-Themed Restaurant

We ended the day at a Western-themed restaurant inside the park. The hallway walls were lined with oval portrait paintings of cartoon bears—stacked side by side the entire length of the corridor. Warm lighting, dark wood paneling, the smell of food after a long day on your feet.

Sitting down after eleven hours of walking, I realized I’d had a genuinely good day.

What I Took Away

I went in skeptical. The ticket price, the lines, the assumption that it’s primarily for families with young children—those preconceptions were real.

But walking through the day, a few things shifted:

The craftsmanship is undeniable. The Beast’s Castle queue is the clearest example: they didn’t just design a ride, they built a fully detailed environment that makes waiting feel like part of the attraction. That level of sustained detail is rare in any industry.

Halloween season is a good time to visit. Late October, after the peak Halloween weekend crowd has moved on, is apparently a sweet spot. Major attractions had 15–50 minute waits, which felt manageable compared to what peak summer or holiday weekends must be like.

The “but it’s for kids” framing missed something. Yes, the content draws from animated films. But the execution is sophisticated enough that adults experience something different—appreciation for the craft, the nostalgia, the sheer commitment of the environment—while kids experience it more directly. Both are real responses to what the park is doing.

Would I go back? Yes.
